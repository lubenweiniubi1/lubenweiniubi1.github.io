3. Expected Q&A (practice these out loud)

Say: "Quite a bit, actually. I finally had time to go deep on some fundamentals I'd been glossing over — system design, how databases actually work under the hood, browser rendering internals… the kind of stuff you never get to when you're on a two-week sprint cycle.

I also beefed up my backend skills. I'd done full-stack at REA Group, but I wanted to be more solid — database design, API architecture, Docker, CI/CD. Got my hands dirtier than before.

And I kept up with AI tools — that's been a bit of an obsession. Built more custom agent skills, refined my Claude Code workflow… the space moves crazy fast so even just keeping up is a job.

So yeah — it wasn't a gap, it was more of an investment. I genuinely feel like I'm a better engineer now than when I left."
Q3: "What kind of role are you looking for?"
Say: "Something senior frontend or full-stack, ideally where I can keep using English day to day. Frontend is home base — that's where I've got the most depth — but I'd miss the backend if I dropped it. At REA Group my title was full-stack engineer and that balance felt about right.

I ended up having to dig into the framework internals, figure out what was going on, research alternative approaches, and basically rewrite the animation module from scratch. It wasn't quick, but we landed at about 70% code reuse between the platforms — which was honestly better than we expected going in. And the approach we figured out, other teams on the project ended up reusing it. That felt pretty satisfying."

Write things down, keep docs readable, don't assume the other person has the same context you do. That one I learned the hard way working with teams in Australia and India — if it's not written down somewhere, it might as well not have happened."

4. Useful Phrases for the Call

ScenarioWhat to say
Can't hear clearly "Sorry, the line isn't great — could you say that again?"
Need a moment to think "That's a good question — give me a second…"
Not sure about something "I'm not a hundred percent sure, but I'd say…"
Move to a related point "That reminds me — there's another thing…"
Caught yourself rambling "Anyway, what I mean is…" / "So the short version is…"
Ending the call "Thanks for your time — really enjoyed talking. Have a good one!"

5. How the Call Will Probably Go

  1. Greeting & small talk (~1 min) — "Hi, how are you?", etc.
  2. Self-introduction (~1 min)
  3. Questions about your work (~3-4 min) — general project talk, your role, team
  4. Follow-ups (~2-3 min) — why you left, what you want next, maybe English level
  5. Wrap-up (~1 min) — any questions from you, goodbye

6. Tips for the Call

Do:
  • Find a quiet room — background noise is the worst on phone calls
  • Keep this page open as a safety net, not a script
  • Smile — it changes your tone even if they can't see you
  • Speak a little slower than you would in Chinese
  • Short sentences > long ones. If you trip, just start the sentence over
  • If you miss something: "Sorry, could you say that again?"
Don't:
  • Don't rush — 8-10 minutes is longer than you think
  • Don't read word for word — they can always tell
  • Don't use technical jargon — nobody's testing your React knowledge
  • Don't obsess over grammar — getting your point across matters way more
  • Don't apologize for your English — you used it at work every day for years
  • Don't freeze if you mess up — just correct yourself and keep going

7. Before You Pick Up

  • Phone charged, volume up, silent mode off
  • Room quiet — windows closed, door shut, family notified
  • Glass of water nearby — your mouth goes dry fast when you're nervous
  • This page + your resume open on screen
  • Say your self-intro out loud at least 3 times — your mouth needs the muscle memory
  • Deep breath. This is an 8-minute conversation in a language you've used at work for nearly five years. You're not being tested on your accent. They just want to see if you can hold a conversation.
